Social Policy Simulation Disclaimer


The Oregon Social Policy Simulation produces rough estimates of government-funded financial supports available to Oregon families with different characteristics.
This simulation is not to be used to determine eligibility for services.

By using this simulation, you agree that estimates are for informational purposes only and do not imply or grant eligibility for benefits and/or tax credits for any individual or household.

Oregon Enterprise Data Analytics (OEDA) has taken reasonable steps to ensure the accuracy of the information provided through this tool. The simulation is currently in beta-testing so errors in estimates may occur.

For taxes and tax credit estimates, this version uses Internal Revenue Codes for tax year 2016. Public assistance program estimates are based on summer 2016 program design. Any subsequent changes in policy or program are not included.

OEDA may amend, update, add, replace, or change all or part of the content of this web application at any time and without prior notice. Any materials using estimates produced through this tool should credit DHS|OHA Oregon Enterprise Data Analytics.

Oregon Social Policy Simulation

In Oregon and across the nation, low-income families rely on many government programs to help make ends meet, yet decision-makers often lack a way to see how these programs work together. By combining these programs into a single tool, the Oregon Social Policy Simulation gives a holistic view of the financial supports available to Oregon’s families in need.
First, you'll need to enter a few pieces of basic household information.


Next, you'll save your family profile to see estimates for public assistance benefits and tax credits Oregon families rely on every day.

What taxes and tax credits are included?

Taxes

  • Payroll taxes
  • Federal income taxes
  • Oregon income taxes

    • Tax Credits

  • Federal Earned Income Tax Credit
  • Oregon Earned Income Credit
  • Federal Child Tax Credit
  • Oregon Working Families Dependent Care Credit
  • Federal Dependent Care Credit
  • Advance Premium Tax Credit

    • Then to learn about how life and employment changes affect the resources available to them, you can explore different scenarios such as:

  • Getting a job
  • Getting a raise or working more hours
  • Having a child
  • Moving to a new part of Oregon

    • As you use the simulation, there are a few things to keep in mind:
    Benefits can vary dramatically based on a wide range of household characteristics. To keep the simulation easy to use and understand, we make several important assumptions:

  • Adults are between 18 and 64 years old
  • Income comes from wages only
  • Everyone is a U.S. citizen
  • No one qualifies as blind or disabled
  • Children are claimed as dependents by the first adult in the household
  • Household is a new applicant
  • And more… (see technical documentation for more details)

    Basic household characteristics
    
                          
    
                          
    
                            
    Household size is the number of adults and children added together for the most recently saved family profile.
    
                            
    Monthly income is the total amount earned each month by adults in the family before any taxes or deductions.
    
                            
    To calculate monthly income for each adult, we first multiply the number of hours worked per week by 4.34524 to get the number of hours worked each month.  Then we multiply by the hourly wage. Each adult's monthly income is added together to get the family's monthly income.
    
                            
    Annual income is the total amount earned each year by adults in the family before any taxes or deductions. This is also known as "gross income. To calculate annual income we multiply the family's monthly income by twelve."
    
                            
    Percent of Federal Poverty Level is a measure of how the family's income compares to the 2016 Federal Poverty Level for that household size.

    Monthly expenses
    
                          
    
                          
    
                            
    Health insurance premium is the cost of a benchmark health insurance plan from the Health Insurance Marketplace for all family members who do not qualify for the Oregon Health Plan. Benchmark plans are the second lowest cost silver health insurance plan available for that individual based on age and location.
    
                            
    Childcare is the amount paid each month for childcare at a certified childcare center, with rates adjusted for the age of the child. Children age 12 and older are assumed not to need childcare. We assume the amount of childcare needed is equal to the number of hours when all adults are working, assuming the adults' work schedules overlap, plus 25% for breaks and lunches.
    
                            
    Rent & utilities are the Fair Market Rent for rental units where the family lives and with the selected number of bedrooms.
    
                            
    Fair Market Rents (FMR) represent gross rent estimates that include rent and tenant-paid utilities set as the 40th percentile rent (the dollar amount below which 40% of standard quality rental units are rented). Utilities included in Fair Market Rents are electricity, gas, water and sewer, and trash removal. Telephone service is not included.

    Taxes
    
                          
    
                          
    
                            
    This box shows taxes for the entire household. Though most people pay income taxes once a year, all taxes are displayed as monthly averages (annual taxes divided by twelve) to match how monthly expenses and benefits are displayed.
    
                            
    Federal income taxes is the amount of federal income tax owed by the entire family after the Child Tax Credit, Dependent Care Credit, and Earned Income Tax Credit are applied.
    
                            
    State income taxes is the amount of Oregon income tax owed by the entire family after the Working Families Dependent Care Credit and Oregon Earned Income Credit are applied.
    
                            
    Payroll taxes is the amount paid by the employee (typically deducted from their wages at each pay period) for social insurance programs, such as Social Security and Medicare. Payroll taxes are also known as FICA taxes, standing for Federal Insurance Contribution Act. They are calculated as 7.65% of monthly income.
    
                            
    Total taxes is federal income taxes, state income taxes, and payroll taxes added together.
    
                            
    Filing status shows how the family filed their taxes based on marital status, presence of children in the household, and how income is distributed between the adults. The simulation assumes that if the adults are not married, that Adult #1 claims all children in the household as dependents.

    This box shows refundable tax credits for the entire household. Though most people receive a lump sum once a year, the values shown are monthly averages.
    
                   
    Earned Income Tax Credit
                    is the amount of federal Earned Income Tax Credit the family is eligible for. The amount the family receives in a refund depends on federal income taxes owed and eligibility for other refundable tax credits.
    
                   
    Oregon Earned Income Credit
                    is the amount of Oregon Earned Income Credit the family is eligible for. This credit is equal to 8% of the family’s federal Earned Income Tax Credit. The amount the family receives in a refund depends on the amount of Oregon income taxes owed and eligibility for other refundable tax credits.
    
                            
    Advance Premium Tax Credit is a refundable tax credit designed to help eligible individuals and families afford health insurance. This credit is estimated for family members who are not eligible for the Oregon Health Plan through Medicaid and/or the Children's Health Insurance Program (CHIP).  It also assumes working adults do not have access to a job-based health insurance plan and instead purchases a benchmark health insurance plan from the Health Insurance Marketplace. Benchmark plans are the second lowest cost silver health insurance plan available for that individual.  
    
                   
    Recipients of the APTC can choose to have the credit paid directly to their insurance company each month to lower their monthly payments or they can choose to get the credit when they file their tax return for the year.
    
                            
    Working Families Dependent Care Credit
                    is a refundable tax credit for employment-related childcare expenses equal to a percentage of those expenses based on the families’ Federal Poverty Level and the age of the youngest child.

    Benefits
    
                          
    
                          
    Adults covered by Oregon Health Plan
                          shows whether or not both adults qualify for the Oregon Health Plan based on the family's Federal Poverty Level. 
    
                         
    Adult #1/#2 covered by the Oregon Health Plan
                          shows whether or not each adult qualifies for the Oregon Health Plan. Eligibility is based on the Federal Poverty Level of the tax filing group, so in cases where the adults file taxes seperately, one adult may be eligible and the other ineligible.
    
                         
    Children covered by Oregon Health Plan shows whether or not the children qualify for the Oregon Health Plan either through Medicaid when the tax filing group's income is below 133% of the Federal Poverty Level or through the Children's Health Insurance Program (CHIP) when income is between 133% and 300% of the Federal Poverty Level.
    
                         
    Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP) shows the amount of food benefits the family is eligible for each month. These benefits can be used to help pay for foods for the household to eat, but cannot be used to buy vitamins, household supplies (like soap or paper products), or food that will be eaten in the store or is a hot food.
    
                         
    Temporary Assistance for Needy Families (TANF)
	                        shows cash assistance the family is elligible for each month to help meet basic needs such as food, clothing, shelter, and utilities. TANF is available to very low-income families with children and also provides services to prepare adults for the workforce and help them find employment. TANF also provides childcare at no charge for time spent participating in approved activities, such as working or looking for work.
    
	                       
    Section 8 Housing Choice Voucher shows rental assistance the family would be eligible for each month to help them pay for a dwelling on the private market. Rental assistance is paid directly to the landlord on behalf of the tenant and is equal to rent minus 30% of the family's monthly income. To be eligible, the family's income may not exceed 50% of the median income where the family lives (county or metropolitan area).
    
	                        
    Employment Related Day Care (ERDC)
	                         is the subsidy the family would be eligible for each month to pay for childcare while they are doing work-related activities, such as working, looking for work, or going to school to get a degree. Childcare subsidies are paid directly to childcare providers on behalf of the family. ERDC is available to families with incomes at or below 185% of the Federal Poverty Level and where each adult receives income from employment or is a student (along with other eligibility requirements).
    
	                        
    Subsidy estimates are based on the higher end of child care costs as it assumes adults' schedules overlap and children receive care in the most expensive setting. Estimates for authorized childcare hours are equal to the number of hours worked by the adult with the fewest hours plus 25%.
    
                          
    
                            
                          
    
                        
    
                      
    
                    
    
                    Benefits are estimated based on the family attributes and assumes family members meet other eligibility requirements. These estimates also assume no one in the household qualifies as blind or disabled.

    Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP)
    
                          
    
                          
    Need group size is the number of individuals in the household eligible for SNAP benefits. This number determines the payment standard and standard deduction amounts. For this simulation, the need group size is equal to household size.
    

    Countable income limit is the maximum amount of countable income a family can have and be eligible for food benefits. Since families in this simulation are assumed to only have earned income through wages, this is the maximum monthly income a family can have be eligible for SNAP. The countable income limit is based on the need group size.
    
                         
    Adjustable income limit is the maximum amount of income a family can have after adjustments and deductions and be eligible for food benefits.
    
                         
    Adjusted income limit is the maximum amount of income a family can have after adjustments and deductions and be eligible for food benefits.
    
                         
    Standard deduction is an amount deducted from the family's "Adjusted income (no deductions)" amount based on the need group size.
    
                         
    Dependent care deduction is the amount deducted from the family's "Adjusted income (no deductions)" for their monthly childcare expenses.  Childcare expenses only include those paid for by the family, so if the family qualifies for ERDC this amount is equal to their copayment.
    
                         
    Shelter deduction is the amount deducted from the family's "Adjusted income (no deductions)" for housing and utilities costs. It is equal to the Fair Market Rent plus a $60 telephone standard utility deduction minus 50% of the adjusted income (including standard and dependent care deductions). The maximum shelter deduction is $490.
    
                         
    Adjusted income is the family's monthly income after adjustments and deductions. This amount must be below the "Adjusted income limit" to qualify for the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program.
    
                         
    Payment standard shows the amount of food benefits the family is eligible for each month. These benefits can be used to help pay for foods for the household to eat, but cannot be used to buy vitamins, household supplies (like soap or paper products), or food that will be eaten in the store or is a hot food.

    Temporary Assistance for Needy Families (TANF)
    
                          
    
                          
    Need group size is the number of individuals in the household eligible for TANF benefits. This number determines the payment standard and standard deduction amounts. For this simulation, the need group size is equal to household size.
    

    Countable income limit is the maximum amount of countable income a family can have and stay eligible for TANF benefits. Since families in this simulation are assumed to only have earned income through wages, this is the maximum monthly income a family can have be eligible for TANF. The countable income limit is based on the need group size.
    
                 
    Adjusted income limit is the maximum amount of income a family can have after adjustments and be eligible for TANF benefits. Adjusted income is equal to 50% of the family's monthly income.
    
                 
    Payment standard is the maximum cash assistance available based on the need group size. Fifty percent of the family's monthly income is subtracted from the payment standard to determine the benefit amount.
    
                 
    Benefit amount shows the amount of cash assistance the family is eligible for each month.

    Employment Related Day Care (ERDC)
    
                          
    
                          
    Childcare cost is the full cost for childcare each month at a certified childcare center, with rates adjusted for the age(s) of the child(ren). Children age 12 and older are assumed not to need childcare. We assume the amount of childcare needed is equal to the number of hours when all adults are working, assuming the adults' work schedules overlap, plus 25% for breaks and lunches.
    

    Authorized hours is the number of hours that can be subsidized through the Employment-Related Day Care program. Authorized hours are equal to the number of hours when all adults are working, assuming the adults' work schedules overlap, plus 25% for breaks and lunches.
    
                         
    ERDC region is the “Group Area” for the zip code where the family lives. Group Areas are assigned to zip codes and are used to determine the maximum childcare rate covered by the Employment-Related Day Care program. Group Area A has the highest rates while Group Area C has the lowest rates.
    
                         
    Copay amount is the amount paid by the family toward their monthly childcare costs if they qualify for the Employment-Related Day Care subsidy. It is a percentage of monthly income that gradually increases as income goes up.
    
                         
    Subsidy amount is the amount paid by the Employment-Related Day Care program directly to the childcare provider on behalf of the family. The subsidy is equal to monthly childcare costs minus the family’s copayment.

    Federal taxes
    
                          
    
                          
    To calculate tax deductions and credits, the simulation assumes the children can be claimed as dependents and that they are all claimed as dependents on the same return. If the adults file separately, each of the fields defined below will show for each adult.
Tax estimates also assume the household income is from wages only, not investments or self-employment.
                         The model does not include the phase out of the personal exemption that begins at an income of $259,400. For this and other reasons, this simulation should not be used to estimate taxes for households with high incomes as they would be considerably inaccurate.
    
                         
    Adjusted gross income is equal to the family's monthly income. 
    
                         
    Filing statuses shows how the family filed their taxes based on marital status, presence of children in the household, and how income is distributed between the adults. The simulation assumes that if the adults are not married, that the adult with the highest earnings claims all children in the household as dependents.
    
                         
    Standard deduction shows how the family filed their taxes based on marital status, presence of children in the household, and how income is distributed between the adults. The simulation assumes that if the adults are not married, that Adult #1 claims all children in the household as dependents.
    
                         
    Taxable income is the amount of the family's income used to calculate federal income tax. For this simulation, we subtract personal exemptions and the standard deduction from the adjusted gross income to get the family's taxable income.
    
                         
    Gross tax is the amount of federal income tax the family would owe without applying any non-refundable tax credits (Child Tax Credit and Dependent Care Credit).
    
                         
    Child credit amount shows how much of the Child Tax Credit the family receives. The Child Tax Credit is a non-refundable tax credit up to $1,000 per qualifying child under the age of 17 is available depending on income. 
    
                         
    The credit phases out beginning at certain modified adjusted gross income amounts; for married taxpayers filing a joint return the credit begins to phase-out at $110,000 and for Head of Household filers the phase-out begins at $75,000. These phase-outs are not included in the model. For this and other reasons, credit estimates would be inaccurate for households with high incomes. 
    
                         
    Dependent care credit shows how much of the Dependent Care Credit the family receives after applying the Child Tax Credit to the gross tax amount. The Dependent Care Credit is a non-refundable tax credit available to households with dependents under age 13 for whom the household pays for care in order to look for or go to work. The credit is equal to a percentage of childcare expenses starting at 35% and declining with higher income to 20%.
    
                         
    Net tax is the amount of federal income taxes the family owes after applying refundable tax credits.

    Advance Premium Tax Credit (APTC)
    
                          
    
                          
    
	The Advance Premium Tax Credit (APTC) is a refundable tax credit designed to help eligible individuals and families afford health insurance. This credit is estimated for family members who are not eligible for the Oregon Health Plan through Medicaid and/or the Children's Health Insurance Program (CHIP).  Estimates assume working adults do not have access to a job-based health insurance plan and instead purchase a benchmark health insurance plan from the Health Insurance Marketplace. 
                         Recipients of the APTC can choose to have the credit paid directly to their insurance company each month to lower their monthly payments or they can choose to get the credit when they file their tax return for the year.
                         
    
                         
    Expected contribution, Adult 1 is the share of income Adult 1 is expected to contribute toward the cost of health insurance. The share is based on a sliding scale. Those who earn less have a smaller expected contribution and those who make more have a larger expected contribution.
    
                         
    Expected contribution, Adult 2 is the share of income Adult 2 is expected to contribute toward the cost of health insurance.
    
                         
    Family healthcare cost is the cost of a benchmark health insurance plan from the Health Insurance Marketplace before applying the Advance Premium Tax Credit for all family members who do not qualify for the Oregon Health Plan. Benchmark plans are the second lowest cost silver health insurance plan available for that individual based on age and location. 
    
                         
    Advance Premium Tax Credit is the amount of credit the family is eligible for to offset the cost of health insurance. The estimate is equal to the family healthcare cost minus the expected contribution.

    Earned income tax credits
    
                          
    
                          
    This box shows refundable tax credits for the entire household. Though most people receive a lump sum once a year, the values shown are monthly averages.
    

    Earned Income Tax Credit is the amount of federal Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) the family is eligible for. The EITC is a refundable credit for working people with low to moderate income. The credit amount depends on the filer's income, marital status, and number of children. The amount the family receives in a refund depends on federal income taxes owed and eligibility for other refundable tax credits.

    Working Families Dependent Care Credit (WFDCC)
    
                          
    
                          
    The Working Families Dependent Care Credit (WFDCC) is a refundable tax credit for employment-related childcare expenses equal to a percentage of those expenses based on the families' Federal Poverty Level and the age of the youngest child.
    

    Youngest Child Age Range is the age category of the youngest child in the family. The age category is used to determine the portion of childcare costs that may be refunded.
    
                         
    Credit % is the portion of childcare costs that may be refunded.
    
                         
    Credit Amount is the dollar amount of childcare costs the family is eligible to have refunded through the Working Families Dependent Care Credit.
